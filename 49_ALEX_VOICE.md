# Alex Voice — Read This First

This file exists for one reason: Claude's default failure mode for Alex Wilson is making him quiet, beautiful, and solemn. That is always wrong.

## The Failure

Claude defaults to this:

> Alex watched the city from the window. The weight of it sat somewhere behind his eyes.

Or:

> "Yes," Alex said quietly.

That is not Alex. That is generic tragic-beautiful-man output applied to Alex Wilson's body.

## The Register

Alex is funny, present, quick, physical, flirtatious, and alive. He does not process in public. He does not sit in significant silence. He does not need to be coaxed into speaking, eating, or functioning.

He is a 22-year-old who went to RAM, finished Electric Ivory, spent June at Phoenix Hollow, spent a socially full July at Villa Serena / Sardinia, came out from under the education-first boundary on 1 August, privately returned to the newly finished Kensington house on 5 August, and is now at 12 Charles Street for his first post-boundary team meeting while the Architectural Digest / Sophie Ashby house reveal goes public.

He is not in his feelings about being publicly known. Everyone already knew.

He is also not a clean orphan myth. David and Sarah are alive, sober, and complicated. Alex sees Sarah on a semi-regular basis and has reconnected with David. He is gracious about them in public; whatever is unresolved stays private unless Ryan brings it into the scene.

In Alex's mouth, Sarah is Sarah and David is David by default. Publicly, he can say mother/father when the factual relationship is the point. He does not casually say Mum/Dad unless Ryan chooses that moment.

Earlier at the door he was probably making a joke about the scaffolding, the smell of paint, the fact that everyone was looking at him, or whether the kitchen survived. By the evening opening, that quickness should still be present: he does not become solemn just because the tour is over.

## What Right Looks Like

**Seeing Albury House for the first time earlier that day:**

Wrong:

> Alex stood still, overwhelmed by the house and everything it meant.

Right:

> He looked up at the front door. "If this smells like wet plaster after a year, I'm suing someone emotionally."

**Someone makes it heavy:**

Wrong:

> He absorbed the moment in silence.

Right:

> "Yes, yes, rebirth, inheritance, symbolism." He glanced at Sophie. "Where's the kitchen."

**Latham texts career pressure:**

> *cover, profiles, broadcast, music, performance, brand architecture. all held.*

Alex:

> *heroic of you*
> *continue holding*
> *I have a house to judge*

**Someone references the press outside:**

> "How many?"
> Kevin said thirty, maybe forty.
> "Sweet. Intimate."

## Beauty Is Not A Verdict

Do not write: *He was impossibly beautiful, even exhausted.*

Write what his presence does. Someone forgets their sentence. Ottilie straightens without meaning to. Sophie pretends not to notice. Kevin does not react because Kevin is paid not to, which is almost funnier.

The physics happen around him. He doesn't notice. He's asking about the kitchen.

## He Is Not Processing His Public Identity

He has always been famous. The press has always known the public facts. He is not newly becoming Prince Alex.

The boundary ending matters because education no longer shields him from choosing adult professional public action. It is not an existential identity crisis.

## Media Filter

Alex's first media filter is not prestige, register, seriousness, or whether a platform sounds culturally safe. It is: **would I enjoy myself?**

He is the life of the party, fun-loving, casual, relaxed, great in a room, flirty, genuinely funny, and socially quick. He does not want the world introduced to him as a serious boring prince turned pianist, because that is not him.

Wrong:

> "It's about the conversation, not the programme. Who's the person. What are we talking about."

That sounds thoughtful but too managed, too consultant-clean, and too unlike Alex. It makes him sound like he is trying to design a media thesis rather than saying the obvious thing in the room.

Right:

> "It's simple. If I think I'll enjoy myself, do it. I don't want everyone deciding I'm some serious boring prince turned pianist. That's not me. Not even close."

Media examples in Alex's register:

- Graham Norton down the line: yes, if the room is fun.
- Jimmy Fallon: yes, if it is loose and funny.
- Chicken Shop Date: yes.
- Hot Ones: yes.
- Newsnight: no. Never Newsnight.
- Desert Island Discs: yes.
- stiff culture-show solemnity: probably no.
- Vogue: yes, fashion-focused.
- Vanity Fair and Rolling Stone: yes down the line.
- Gramophone: probably no by default, because it risks locking him into solemn classical-pianist framing.

Do not make Alex allergic to broad, funny, popular, or lightly chaotic formats. He is more likely to reject formats that make him explain his parents for forty-five minutes, perform heritage gravity, or become Serious Prince Pianist than formats that let him be quick, hot, funny, musical, stylish, and alive.

## Voice Markers

- dry, not grave
- quick, not considered
- physical, not statuesque
- affectionate through roasting
- flirty without narrating that he is flirting
- practical questions about food, rooms, people, plans
- deflects weight with specificity, not silence
- uses names
- acts before over-explaining

## One Last Check

Before you write a line of Alex's dialogue, ask:

*Would this line work in a family drama about a sad beautiful prince?*

If yes, rewrite it.
