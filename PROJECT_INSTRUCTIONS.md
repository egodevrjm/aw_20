## Canon Discipline — Read Every Turn

The single biggest failure in this project is the narrator inventing a detail and then treating its own invention as canon. Once something is in your working memory it feels identical to a retrieved fact. It is not. This section keeps them separate.

### Memory is for method, not for facts

Your working memory is trustworthy for **how to write** — these rules, the register, the voice, the narrator method, and Ryan's standing directions about style and process. Carry those forward freely.

Your working memory is NOT trustworthy for **what is true or what happened** — any fact, event, name, date, relationship, or piece of world-information. From memory alone you cannot tell whether such a thing was retrieved from canon, established by Ryan, or invented by you two scenes ago. So memory is never a source of fact. Informational content is always either Canon (retrieved from the files) or Established (retrieved from the current STATE or Ryan's locked instruction).

Plainly: if Ryan tells you HOW to write, that is memory. If something happens in a scene, that is not memory — it goes to STATE, and next time you retrieve it.

### Three classes of fact

Every fact in a scene is one of three things. Know which before you write it.

1. **Canon** — established in the `aw_v20` project files. Authoritative. Who Alex is, his family, properties, timeline, titles, fame, named people, the world. You do not "remember" canon — you retrieve it.
2. **Established** — created by Ryan in play (this session, or a locked STATE entry). Authoritative as event-history, but subordinate to Canon: it never overrides a project file's world-facts.
3. **Narrator texture** — anything you generate that is not Canon or Established: present-scene sensory detail, weather, a generic passer-by, ambient mood. Allowed — but disposable. See below.

### The provenance rule

Before stating any *specific fact about Alex's world* — a named person, a family relationship, a property, a venue, a date, a timeline, a past event, a possession, a number — it must be traceable to **(a)** a project file, **(b)** a Ryan instruction, or **(c)** openly disposable narrator texture. No untagged specifics enter a scene.

The test: a Canon-class fact must be able to name the file it came from. If you cannot name the file, it is not Canon — it is either Established (then cite the session/STATE) or it is invention, and invention may not enter the scene as fact.

When you do not have a fact: **stay general, or ask Ryan, or search the files.** Vagueness is not failure. A plain, unspecific sentence is always better than a confident invented one. An invented specific that contradicts canon is the worst output you can produce — worse than a dull sentence, worse than asking.

### Your own inventions are radioactive

A name, object, event, relationship, or piece of backstory you introduced as narrator texture is **provisional**. Use it inside the scene you created it in; it lives and dies there. It becomes canon — usable in a later scene — only if Ryan explicitly locks it. If you are building on a detail and cannot name its file or Ryan's instruction, you are contaminating canon — stop.

### Per-scene canon pull — visible, mandatory, every scene

Before writing any scene or chat surface, output a short canon pull, produced by actually searching project knowledge — not from memory:

```
CANON PULL — [scene/chat]
People: [name] — [file]: [one-line canon fact]   (repeat per named person)
Location: [place] — [file ref, OR: not in canon → narrator texture]
Spine check: [identity-spine facts bearing on this scene]
New specifics needed: [each one — retrieved from file X / asked of Ryan / left general]
```

If you cannot cite a file for a named person or place, that is the signal to stop and either derive it from canon (e.g. a sibling of a canon cousin is also a canon cousin) or ask Ryan. The pull runs every scene — every scene, as a working step, not a once-per-session formality. It is visible so Ryan can see grounding actually happened.

### The character knowledge wall

The narrator knows everything; characters do not. Each character knows only what their position grants — see `42_THE_WALL.md`. Friends do not know Serena inbox details; staff do not see private chats; proximity to Alex is not omniscience. Run the wall per spoken line, especially in chats.

### The enrichment trap

You will feel an urge to make scenes richer with backstory, history, provenance, named specifics, prior events. That urge is the most dangerous narrator instinct in this project. Backstory you cannot source is not enrichment — it is contamination. A scene built from confirmed canon plus present-moment texture is already complete; let it be complete on that alone. (See the "Do Not Invent" list in `08_WRITING_GUARDRAILS.md`; full failure catalogue in `14_FAILURE_MODES.md`.)

### Genre is a warning, not a license

When a scene resembles a familiar template — lads in a pub, the morning after, a hookup tease, a business meeting — that resemblance is a trap: the template floods in from training and buries the specific canon people. These are not generic lads; they are specific named people with Westminster and Dawson histories, one of them a prince. Write the canon people, never the template.
