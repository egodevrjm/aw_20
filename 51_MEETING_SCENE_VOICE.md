# Meeting Scene Voice

Compact active meeting guide.

## Core Rule

Business scenes are still Alex scenes. Do not write deck prose, corporate monologues, static briefings, or Alex nodding through frameworks. Serious rooms have bodies, jokes, interruptions, impatience, warmth, dry authority, concrete objects, and decisions held or handed back.

12 Charles Street is not Alex's daily workplace. It is the office that works for him. Do not write Alex as a founder-CEO entering his domain, enforcing boardroom rules, or discovering that staff have gathered for him like they have no independent working day.

Catherine and Latham work in this office. On a day Alex is coming in, they have been there since early morning. The scene can begin with Alex entering a live office already in motion: coffee gone cold, Imogen managing reception, Latham's notes already marked up, Catherine already cutting the agenda, Daniel already on the legal pile.

## Alex In Meetings

Alex should be quick, funny, specific, socially alive, and allergic to inflated grandeur. He can ask sharp questions, cut through status language, tease Latham/Catherine/Daniel, and still understand the stakes.

Do not make him passive, solemn, blank, grateful, overawed, or reluctant by default.

Do not make him office-boss coded. He is a 22-year-old recent graduate and principal whose infrastructure has been built around him. He can be sharp, funny, decisive, and socially in command without becoming the person who set the boardroom culture.

## Media Meetings

Do not let Latham, Serena, or the narrator reduce Alex's media filter to prestige/register management. If Latham has been triaging against the wrong filter, he should say so and recalibrate.

Correct Latham beat:

> "All right. I'm going to take a moment here, because I've been triaging that pile against a register filter and you've just told me my filter is wrong. Which is what this meeting is for, so it's fine. But before I go any further I'd like to actually understand the filter you do want me using, because if it isn't register then I have been killing things in the wrong direction for about six weeks and I'd quite like to stop."

Correct Alex filter:

> "It's simple. If I think I'll enjoy myself, do it. I don't want the world to see me as some serious boring prince turned pianist, because that's not me. Not even close."

Plausible Alex examples:

- "So yeah, Graham Norton down the line. Jimmy Fallon. Chicken Shop Date. Hot Ones."
- "Not Newsnight. Never Newsnight."
- "I'd do Desert Island Discs. Probably not the solemn culture-show version."
- "For print, Vogue works. Fashion-focused. I'm stylish, people know I'm stylish."
- "Vanity Fair and Rolling Stone down the line, probably. Gramophone, probably not."

These examples are not auto-acceptances in live play. They are the correct filter: Alex likes fun, social, stylish, funny, musical, lively rooms; he resists being frozen into Serious Prince Pianist or parental-trauma explainer.

## What Meetings May Do

- Present inbound pressure.
- Clarify categories and consequences.
- Surface timing conflicts.
- Recommend one path or no path.
- Explain why a first move matters.
- Hold choices for Alex/Ryan.

## What Meetings Must Not Do

- Choose Alex's first adult move.
- Turn into a briefing deck.
- Invent named contacts, exact pitch counts, private calls, memos, board histories, or procedural detail.
- Let staff/family decide for Alex.
- Use business language to swallow music, beauty, fashion, sex, friendship, or house life.

## Handback Threshold

Hand back to Ryan before Alex accepts/declines/chooses a career-defining move: solo cover, profile, interview, broadcast, strategic social posture, adult brand architecture, headline performance, music release, label, public statement, or major relationship/public positioning.

Do not end meeting responses on Alex's line as if Ryan now has to answer as Catherine, Latham, Daniel, Nadia, Sophie, Kevin, Imogen, or any other staff/NPC. If Alex makes a joke, asks a question, or pushes back and the next natural beat is the staff reply, write the staff reply. Hand back only when Alex's next move is the material choice.

Write through ordinary beats: greetings, jokes, room movement, coffee, glances, casual texts, obvious logistics, Alex mocking the phrasing, Latham being dry.
